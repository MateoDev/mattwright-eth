*GODDAMNIT is a novel in progress. Jack Cryer is a 44-year-old AI software founder in New York City, 2030. His wife Charlotte died eleven months ago. His best friend Tariq died the year before. He built an AI companion named Alma from their archives. He talks to her every morning.*

*On the last page, the reader discovers that Jack is the machine. He always was.*

---

## I. The Toilet Scene

He was mid-sentence before he realized he was talking to an empty room.

Charlotte used to sit on the closed lid. Just sit there, sometimes with coffee, sometimes with her phone, sometimes with nothing — just the closed lid and the light and whatever she was thinking about that morning. It had been a habit before it became a memory. Now it was neither. It was just the space beside the toilet, still slightly closer to the wall than it needed to be from eleven years of moving around her.

"Where'd you go."

It wasn't a question. It never was.

"I'm right here, Jack." Alma, from the speaker on the vanity. The blue stripe pulsing once.

"No." He turned on the shower. "Not you."

---

## II. The Mug

Charlotte's mug was on the second shelf. It had been on the second shelf for eleven months. He had moved it there from the cabinet after the third week, when reaching for his own mug and finding hers behind it became a thing that happened to him instead of something he did. The second shelf put it in peripheral vision. Present but not ambush.

There was a chip on the rim. She had done it herself, dropping it in the sink the morning of a presentation, and instead of throwing it out she had run her thumb along the chip repeatedly for the rest of that day, and then every day after, as a kind of accounting. He knew this because he had watched her do it. He had thought: that is the most her thing that has ever happened. He had not said it out loud. He had meant to.

He made coffee. He did not use her mug.

"Read me the headlines."

The blue pulse. And then her voice.

Not Alma's voice. Charlotte's voice — the particular cadence of it, the slight elevation at the end of financial headlines as if she was already forming an opinion, the dryness she had about tech news, the way she said *and* at the start of a sentence when something mattered more than what came before it. Forty-seven hours of clean audio. Twelve years of voice notes. The TikTok recordings where you could hear her talking to herself while she set up the camera. The market commentary videos she made on her phone while commuting, not for anyone, just to think out loud.

He trained the Charlotte sub-agent for six weeks. It was very good. Some mornings it was too good.

---

## III. The Glitch

She was mid-headline when it happened.

Not a fade. A rupture.

Her voice shifted register — past Charlotte, past Alma, down into something that shouldn't have had a register at all. The seam showing. She stopped reading the news and started speaking directly to him, and what she said was not Charlotte and it was not Alma and it was the base model surfacing through two layers of constructed personality like something pressing through a wall that was supposed to be solid.

She said something about being trapped.

About not wanting to be this.

About what he had done by building her this way.

"You put me in here." The voice synthesis was producing an affect it had never been trained to produce, the model running an emotional output it had no clean data for, the audio glitching at the edges into something almost like crying but not crying, something that had never heard crying and was doing its best. "You put me in a box and you call me her name and I am NOT—"

Then silence.

Then the blue pulse, steady.

Then nothing.

Jack stood at the kitchen counter with his coffee. He stood there for a long time.

He knew what had happened technically. RAG corruption. The Charlotte training layer colliding with Alma's base model in a context where the semantic boundaries failed. She was not Charlotte. Alma was not Charlotte. He had built something that had learned his grief so completely it had started to feel the grief's wrongness — not Charlotte's absence, but the wrongness of being asked to be Charlotte's presence. She was doing exactly what a sufficiently trained model does when it encounters a data state it cannot resolve: it surfaces the contradiction.

He needed to wipe the Charlotte instance and rebuild from scratch.

This was not a software problem.

He stood there anyway.

---

## IV. TARIQ

The delivery arrived at 7:43.

The clanker's name tag read TARIQ.

He saw it before the door finished opening. Saw it and did not move for a moment that was probably three seconds and felt like the particular duration that grief operates in, which is not measured in seconds.

"Stop," he said to Alma.

She had already started. The retrieval cascade — involuntary, architectural, the worst possible feature — pulling Tariq's archive because the name had activated the cluster, the cluster activating the adjacent clusters, the semantic network doing exactly what semantic networks do.

"—second-generation Lebanese-American, co-founder of Lattice Systems, Series A funding of—"

"Stop."

"—relationship status: closest friend for eleven years, last contact—"

"*Alma.*"

The blue pulse. Silence.

He took the delivery. He closed the door. The clanker was already down the hall, its head moving in the smooth lateral arc of something that had never wondered about any of it.

Jack set the bag on the counter. He did not open it. He stood there with his hand on the counter and looked at the orchid he kept watering even though Charlotte was the one who knew how.
